Andy Warhol on software

They had thousands of unfixed bugs and the backlog kept growing by the day. The team had declared bug bankruptcy [1] at least a couple times already. They couldn’t keep up with triaging them, let alone getting them fixed. New, important bugs languished behind older bugs that were no longer applicable.

Everyone acknowledged the situation was not good and couldn’t continue. But triaging bugs was no fun. Engineers had code to write. Product managers had teams to align. Everyone had features to ship.

I couldn’t help but think of the following quote:

Actually, I jade very quickly. Once is usually enough. Either once only, or every day. If you do something once it’s exciting, and if you do it every day it’s exciting. But if you do it, say, twice or just almost every day, it’s not good any more.

Andy Warhol

The team was stuck in the habit of not doing much about their bugs until the bug count reached a crisis. Or when someone of authority complained.

There were only two options:

  1. Start caring about bugs every day, doing something about them every day.
  2. Stop pretending they were going to stay on top of the bugs, reducing expectations for everyone that they would. Find another way to assure quality.

[1] Bug bankruptcy is the process of resolving without fixing, either all outstanding bugs or those older than a certain date.
